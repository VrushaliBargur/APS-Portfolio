Introduction

With over 200 million members worldwide, Prime has become a cornerstone of Amazon's ecosystem, enhancing customer loyalty and driving engagement across its platforms.

Classic Algorithms Used in Amazon Prime

1. Graph Algorithms – Dijkstra's Algorithm

Use: Used to find the shortest or most efficient delivery routes. Helps optimize time and cost by calculating shortest paths or solving complex vehicle routing problems.

Solving Performance Bottlenecks with Dijkstra's Algorithm

Dijkstra’s Algorithm helps Amazon Prime optimize delivery routes by finding the shortest or fastest paths from warehouses to customer locations. This reduces delivery time, lowers fuel costs, avoids traffic delays, and ensures fast, reliable shipping — a core promise of Prime.

Time: O((V + E) log V)
Space: O(V)

2. LRU Caching

Use:Manage which items to keep in cache to speed up content delivery. Evict less important data to free space for new data.

Solving Performance Bottlenecks with LRU Caching

LRU (Least Recently Used) Caching enables Amazon Prime to deliver frequently accessed content—like popular videos or frequently viewed products—faster by storing them in memory. This reduces server load, decreases latency, and improves user experience during high traffic periods.

Time: O(1)
Space: O(capacity)

3. 0/1 Knapsack (Dynamic Programming)

Use: Used to allocate resources efficiently, e.g., decide which products to stock or ship to maximize profit or minimize cost.

Solving Performance Bottlenecks with 0/1 Knapsack

Amazon Prime leverages the 0/1 Knapsack algorithm to optimize inventory loading and delivery logistics by selecting the most valuable combination of items that fit within capacity constraints (like delivery vans or warehouse space). By prioritizing items based on demand, profitability, or urgency, this algorithm helps Amazon maximize resource utilization, ensure fast delivery of high-priority goods, and reduce transportation and storage inefficiencies—contributing directly to Prime’s promise of fast and reliable service.

Time: O(nW)
Space: O(nW)

4. TF-IDF for Search Ranking

Use: Used in search engines to rank and retrieve relevant content quickly.

Solving Performance Bottlenecks with 0/1 Knapsack

Amazon Prime uses the TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) algorithm to enhance search accuracy in its platform by ranking products, videos, and content based on relevance to user queries. By scoring how important a term is in a specific document relative to a large corpus, TF-IDF helps prioritize more meaningful results over generic ones. This ensures users find the most relevant content quickly—improving search efficiency, content discovery, and overall user satisfaction on Prime.

Time: O(N)
Space: O(T + D)

5. Load Balancing – Round Robin

Use: Distribute requests efficiently to prevent overload on any single server.

Solving Performance Bottlenecks with 0/1 Knapsack

Load balancing using the Round Robin algorithm helps Amazon Prime efficiently distribute incoming user requests across multiple servers. By sequentially assigning each new request to the next server in a cyclic order, Round Robin prevents any single server from becoming a bottleneck due to overload. This balanced distribution ensures optimal resource utilization, reduces response time, and maintains system stability during high traffic, thereby solving performance bottlenecks and providing a smooth and reliable user experience.

Time: O(1)
Space: O(1)
